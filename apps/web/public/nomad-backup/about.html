Nigeria spent over ₦130 trillion in the past eight years. Almost none of it became capital. Most of it simply disappeared — into airtime, betting, impulse transfers, and daily habits that felt small and added up to millions.

We built Nomad because the problem is not that Nigerians don't earn enough. It is that the money moves too fast, in the wrong direction, without record.

We are not a savings app. We are not a bank. We are behavioral infrastructure — built to slow money down, record what happens to it, and create proof of discipline where none existed before.

The origin

1841

In 1841, Lewis Tappan noticed a problem. As America's economy expanded, merchants were doing business with strangers — people they had no history with, no community knowledge of, no way to evaluate. The question every creditor had was the same: can this person be trusted with money?

Tappan's answer was simple. He built a network of local observers who watched merchants and wrote down what they actually saw. Did they pay on time? Did they live beyond their means? Did they honor commitments under pressure? That behavioral observation, written down and shared, became the Mercantile Agency — and eventually the American credit system that hundreds of millions of people participate in today.

They did not invent wealth. They invented proof of behavior. Everything that followed — mortgages, business loans, economic mobility — was built on top of that proof system.

Nigeria in 2024 has the same unsolved problem Tappan saw in 1841. Over 40 million adults are credit-invisible — not because they are undisciplined, but because there is no system watching and recording their financial behavior. Banks reject them not because they can't manage money, but because they can't prove they can.

Nomad is Tappan's insight rebuilt for the individual. Instead of a local reporter watching a merchant, the protocol itself is the witness. Every lock completed. Every relock honored. Every impulse resisted. Written down permanently. Yours to show.

The outcome we are building toward is not a savings product. It is a proof system — one that lets 40 million Nigerians show lenders, landlords, employers, and institutions exactly who they are with money. Built not from wealth, but from behavior.

What we believe

Four things we
know to be true.

01

Access is the problem. Not discipline.

Most people do not have a spending problem. They have an access problem. When money is instantly available, it is instantly vulnerable. Removing instant access does not require willpower — it requires architecture. Nomad is that architecture.

02

Behavior is more valuable than balance.

A person who locks ₦50,000 for 30 days and does not touch it is demonstrating something that a bank balance cannot show. Consistency under constraint is the signal that predicts future financial reliability. This is what we measure. This is what lenders need and currently cannot see.

03

The system should record accurately — not judge.

We do not penalize people for being human. Early unlocks are permitted. Mistakes are allowed. What changes is the record. Every action is logged permanently — the good and the bad. A clean record built over time is worth more than a perfect record built over a week. Lenders see the history, not just the score.

04

Wealth passes through systems, not accounts.

The 1% do not keep their capital liquid. Illiquidity is their strategy. Private vaults, structured trusts, locked positions — the mechanisms of controlled access have always been the tools of people who build generational wealth. Nomad translates those mechanisms into infrastructure that works at ₦1,000 entry, not $10 million.
